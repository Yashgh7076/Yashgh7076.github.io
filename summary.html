Education

  • MS., Clemson University, Computer Engineering.

    MS Thesis: Segmentation and recognition of eating gestures from wrist motion using deep learning. Implemented the first deep learning neural network in the world to simultaneously identify and label (semantic segmentation) periods of wrist motion into predefined categories based on specific set of activities occuring in each period of motion.

    Wrist motion was recorded using IMU sensors fitted in a watch-like device, and it can be classified into distinct catgories or 'gestures' based on the activity occuring in a particular period of time, such as moving food from the plate to the mouth ('bite'), moving a cup towards the mouth ('drink'), prepare a morsel of food, or dip food in gravy/sauce ('utensiling'), resting in between different activities ('rest') and all other activities collectively termed as 'other'.

    Our model achieved close to 80% average accuracy per meal at identifying all eating related gestures in the meals considered here: Clemson Cafeteria Dataset with close to 20% standard deviation on average per meal for each gesture type related to eating. This indicates that the implemented model is robust against changes in wrist motion for a large group of people, and can be raliably used to track eating activity in people. For more information, please visit the project description, Clemson University MS Thesis.
